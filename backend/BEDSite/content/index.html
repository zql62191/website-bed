What is binge eating
disorder (B.E.D.)?

B.E.D., now a distinct disorder, is defined as recurring episodes (≥ once weekly, for at least 3 months) of consuming a large amount of food in a short time, compared with others. Patients feel a lack of control during a binge and marked distress over their binge eating. They typically experience shame and guilt, among other symptoms, about their bingeing. Compensatory behaviors are not observed like those associated with bulimia nervosa.1

These are not the complete diagnostic criteria. Diagnosis should be based upon a complete evaluation of the patient that confirms the criteria for B.E.D. established in DSM-5®.

Binge eating disorder (B.E.D.) affects
≈2.8 million US adults*2,3

Nearly 3 million US adults have B.E.D.
Estimated 12-month prevalence in National Comorbidity Survey Replication of US adults aged ≥18 years, extrapolated to full US population aged ≥18 years.2,3
B.E.D. is the most common eating disorder among US adults and more than twice as prevalent as bulimia nervosa and anorexia nervosa combined2

The gender ratio is far less skewed in B.E.D. than in bulimia nervosa.1

The essential features
of binge eating disorder (B.E.D.)

Individuals with B.E.D. must meet all of the
following criteria1:

At least once per week for 3 months, they experience a loss of control over eating and consume an abnormally large amount of food in a short period of time compared with what others might eat in the same amount of time.

These episodes feature at least 3 of the following: consuming food faster than normal; consuming food until uncomfortably full; consuming large amounts of food when not hungry; consuming food alone due to embarrassment; feeling disgusted, depressed or guilty after eating a large amount of food.

Overall, they feel significant distress about their binge eating.

They do not show regular compensatory behavior associated with bulimia nervosa, nor do they binge eat solely during an episode of bulimia nervosa or anorexia nervosa.

Diagnosis should be based upon a complete evaluation of the patient
that confirms the criteria for B.E.D. established in DSM-5®.

What are common triggers
of binge eating episodes?

Negative affect most commonly precedes a bingeing episode.1

Other triggers include1:

  • Interpersonal stressors
  • Dietary restraint
  • Feeling bad about body weight, body shape, and food
  • Feeling bored

A number of functional consequences are affiliated
with binge eating disorder (B.E.D.)1

  • Problems with adapting to
    social roles
  • Quality of life and life satisfaction degradation due to health issues
  • Heightened medical mortality and morbidity
  • Heightened use of health care resources as compared to BMI-matched controls
  • Potential to gain weight and could lead to obesity

reported functional

reported severe

While the exact cause is unknown,

Research suggests B.E.D. may have a neurobiological basis
with genetic and environmental risk
factors6-12

Prevailing theories that may support a neurobiological basis for B.E.D.:

Reward center and impulsivity dysregulation theory

Adults with B.E.D. have shown reduced activity in impulse control–related
regions of the brain6

Neurotransmitter
dysregulation theory

Dopamine signaling converts sensory stimuli into processes that may
generate or enhance “wanting” of a particular food7-9

Endogenous μ-opioid signaling dysfunction theory

Opioid signaling converts sensory stimuli into processes that generate
or enhance “liking” of a particular food8,9

Research also suggests that risk factors for B.E.D. may include:

Genetic influences7,10

  • B.E.D. appears to run in families, which may reflect additive genetic influences
  • Genetic differences in neurobiological components of the brain’s reward center have been observed

Environmental and social influences

  • In a case-controlled retrospective assessment of 162 women with B.E.D.,
    stressful life events were reported more frequently in the year preceding B.E.D. onset than in non-B.E.D. controls11
  • In a nationally representative survey of 5,702 adults, of the men (n=30) and women (n=75) who reported B.E.D., 98% and 90%, respectively, reported experiencing some form of trauma*12

Types of trauma included combat experience,
life-threatening accident, major natural disaster, and physical or sexual assault.12

Recognizing binge eating disorder (B.E.D.) in adults

B.E.D. is defined as recurring episodes (at least once weekly for
3 months) of consuming a large amount of food in a short period of time, compared to what others might eat, with episodes characterized by experiencing a lack of control and significant distress regarding binge eating, among other criteria.1

Please register to receive the DSM-5® diagnostic criteria and additional information from Shire about B.E.D. in adults.

B.E.D. is different from
bulimia nervosa

It is not associated with compensatory behaviors such as purging or excessive exercise, or with
dietary restriction, although frequent dieting may be reported.1

B.E.D. is different from overeating

While overeating is a challenge for many Americans, recurrent binge eating is much less common,
far more severe, and is associated with significant physical and psychological problems.13

B.E.D. is different from obesity

Compared with weight-matched obese adults without B.E.D., obese adults with B.E.D. eat more
calories in studies of how people eat and show1:

  • More functional impairment
  • Greater self-reported distress
  • Impaired quality of life
  • More coexisting psychiatric conditions

BED occurs in normal-weight, overweight, and obese individuals2

Based on a nationally representative survey of US adults aged ≥18 years*2:

19%

are normal weight
(BMI 18.5-24.9)

36%

are overweight
(BMI 25-29.9)
